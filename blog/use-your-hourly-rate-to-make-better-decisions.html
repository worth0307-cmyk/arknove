Use Your Hourly Rate to Make Better Decisions

Once you know what an hour is worth, a surprising number of everyday choices stop being hard. Here is the framework.

June 8, 2026 8 min read Productivity

Most decisions about money and time feel like matters of taste. Should you pay someone to clean the apartment? Take the cheaper flight with the long layover? Spend an evening on a side project? We agonize over these because they seem to have no right answer. But many of them do — and the answer falls out the moment you know your real hourly rate.

The idea is simple: once an hour of your time has a price, you can compare time and money on the same scale. Here is how to use that in practice.

The core rule: buy back hours that cost less than your rate

Say your effective hourly rate — after tax and the hidden costs we covered in a previous guide — is 25. Now any task you can hand off for less than 25 an hour is, in pure economic terms, worth handing off, provided you would actually use the reclaimed time for something more valuable.

A two-hour task you dread, that someone will do for 30 total, costs you 15 an hour to be rid of. Against a 25 rate, that is a clear win. The grocery delivery fee that saves you ninety minutes, the laundry service, the accountant who untangles your taxes in a fraction of the time it would take you — run each through the same test.

If buying back an hour costs less than an hour earns you, you are not being lazy. You are doing arithmetic.

The honest caveat: this only works if you reinvest the freed time in something worth more than the cost — rest, higher-value work, or people. Pay someone to clean so you can stare at your phone, and you have simply spent money. The rule buys time; what you do with it is the actual decision.

The reverse test: is this extra work worth it?

The rule runs backwards too. A side project, an overtime shift, or a freelance gig all promise money in exchange for hours. Divide the pay by the real hours involved — including preparation, admin, and the recovery time afterwards — and compare it to your rate.

A weekend job that pays a flat 200 but eats twelve real hours pays under 17 an hour. If your day job rate is 25, you are working your weekend for a pay cut. Sometimes that is still worth it — for the experience, the contacts, or because you enjoy it. But now you are choosing with open eyes instead of being flattered by a lump sum.

Big decisions: the commute is a pay cut

The framework scales up to the largest choices. A job offer with a 10% higher salary but an extra hour of daily commuting is not a 10% raise. Those five extra hours a week are unpaid, and they push your real hourly rate down. Convert the new salary against the new total committed hours and the "raise" can evaporate — or even turn negative.

The same logic clarifies the move closer to work, the choice between two apartments, and whether remote work that saves a commute is effectively a substantial pay rise. It usually is.

Small decisions: permission to stop feeling guilty

Some of the most useful applications are the smallest. People carry a surprising amount of guilt about minor conveniences — the taxi instead of three buses, the prepared meal instead of cooking after a long day. Run them through the rate and the guilt often turns out to be misplaced.

If a taxi saves you forty minutes for the price of fifteen minutes of your time, and you are tired, that is not indulgence; it is a sensible trade. The framework does not tell you to spend more. It tells you which spending actually buys back something worth more than it costs, so you can do it without the nagging sense that you should have suffered through the cheaper option.

How to make it automatic

The trick is to make your rate familiar enough that it surfaces on its own. This is where watching your earnings in real time helps. When you have spent time with a live number — seeing that an hour is, concretely, this much — the figure stops being theoretical. You start to feel the price of an hour the way you feel the price of a coffee, and the comparisons happen automatically.

That is the quiet payoff of knowing your rate. Not a life of relentless optimization, but a steady, low-effort clarity: a sense of what your time is worth that turns dozens of small dilemmas into simple arithmetic, and leaves you with more of the one resource the whole exercise was about.
