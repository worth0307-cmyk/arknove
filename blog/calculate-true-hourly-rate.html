How to Calculate Your True Hourly Rate

Your real hourly wage isn't your salary divided by hours worked. It's almost always lower. Here's the honest math.

May 24, 2026 9 min read Finance

Ask any salaried worker what their hourly rate is and they'll do a quick mental calculation: annual salary divided by 2,080 (the standard work-year of 40 hours times 52 weeks). Easy. A $70,000 salary becomes about $33.65 an hour. A $120,000 salary becomes $57.69. Done.

The problem is that this number is almost completely fictional. It assumes you only work the hours you're paid for, that getting to work is free, that you'd own the same clothes and eat the same lunches whether or not you had a job, and that taxes don't exist. None of that is true.

The financial advisor Vicki Robin and the late Joe Dominguez introduced a concept they called the real hourly wage in their 1992 book Your Money or Your Life. The idea is simple: your true hourly rate is what you actually take home, after subtracting every cost of having the job, divided by every hour the job actually consumes.

When you do this math, the number usually drops by 30 to 50 percent. Sometimes more. This is the figure you should be using when you decide whether a $40 lunch is "worth it" or whether you should take that promotion that adds two hours to your commute.

The formula

The honest version of hourly rate looks like this:

True hourly rate = (Take-home pay − Job-related costs) ÷ (Paid hours + Unpaid time the job consumes)

Both halves of this equation are usually wrong in the standard calculation. The top is too high because it ignores costs. The bottom is too low because it ignores time. Let's fix both.

The hidden subtractions: what your job actually costs you

These are things you would not be spending money on if you didn't have your specific job. Some are obvious, some are sneaky.

1. Income tax

You don't actually earn your gross salary. After federal, state, and local taxes, plus Social Security and Medicare in the US (or equivalent elsewhere), you might keep 65 to 80 percent of the headline number. Use your actual take-home pay, not your gross.

2. Commuting

Gas, transit passes, car maintenance, car insurance (the portion attributable to commuting), parking, tolls. If you wouldn't own a car without your job, the entire cost of the car belongs here. A typical American worker spends $2,000 to $5,000 a year just getting to and from work.

3. Work-specific clothes

The suits you don't wear on weekends. The shoes that wear out from standing all day. The dry cleaning. If you work in tech, this might be zero. If you work in law or banking, it can be thousands.

4. Work meals

The coffee you buy on the way in. The lunch you eat out because you didn't have time to pack one. The drinks after work with colleagues. Estimate honestly — most people underestimate this by half.

5. Decompression and recovery

This one is uncomfortable. The therapy. The massages. The expensive vacations you "need" because work has drained you. The wine that helps you fall asleep on Sunday night. Some of this would exist anyway. Some of it wouldn't.

6. Childcare and pet care during work hours

If you wouldn't need it without the job, it counts.

7. Job-adjacent education

Certifications, conferences (the part not reimbursed), the MBA you took partly because the job demanded it.

The hidden additions: time you don't count as work

The denominator is the part most people miss entirely. You don't work 40 hours a week. You work 40 paid hours, plus all the unpaid time the job consumes.

  • Commute time. If you commute 45 minutes each way, that's 7.5 hours a week you cannot use for anything else. It is, functionally, work.
  • Getting ready for work. The shower, the outfit, the makeup — the version of yourself you have to assemble for the office. Not all of it counts, but some of it does.
  • Work after work. Email at 9 PM. Slack on Sunday. The half-hour decompressing before you can be human again. The hours you're physically off but mentally still at the desk.
  • Travel for work. The whole trip, including the airport hours and the time zone recovery.

The gap

This person thought they made $43 an hour. They actually make $20. Roughly half of what they thought. Every "is it worth it" calculation they made in their head was wrong by a factor of two.

What you do with this number

Some people find this depressing. We'd argue it's the opposite: it gives you back a sense of proportion.

That $80 dinner is not "two hours of work." It's four. That $300 jacket is not a half-day. It's a full one. The hour you spent arguing with customer service over a $30 refund did not, in fact, pay for itself.

Conversely: rest, which you used to feel guilty about, is one of the only things that doesn't cost you anything. Walks are free. Reading is free. Sitting in a park is free. Once the real hourly rate becomes visible, the relative price of spending versus not spending shifts.

This is also a powerful tool for big decisions. Two job offers? Compare true hourly rates, not gross salaries — including the unpaid hours each will demand. A promotion with longer hours? Run the numbers. Sometimes the new "raise" is actually a pay cut once you account for the time it consumes.

The honest hourly rate doesn't tell you what to do. It just tells you the price tag on what you're already doing.
