What Happens When You Make Time Visible

The behavioral science behind why watching your salary tick up changes how you think — and why it isn't quite the same as any other money tracker you've used.

May 24, 2026 8 min read Psychology

The first time someone uses Pulse, there's usually a small, surprised moment about ten seconds in. Not when they see the per-second number — that's what they expected. The surprise comes when the per-minute number ticks over for the first time and they realize that, in the time it took them to read a paragraph, they were paid something. A real amount. Not a hypothetical.

People describe this moment in remarkably similar ways. "Huh." Or: "Wait, really?" Or just a quiet pause. Whatever the math says, the feeling is the same — a small click in perception, like a slide projector advancing one frame.

This isn't a productivity gain. It isn't motivation. It's something else. And it turns out there's a name for what's happening.

The salience problem

Behavioral economists distinguish between two ways of knowing a fact. There's cognitive knowing — the kind where, if asked, you could recite the number. And there's experiential knowing — the kind where the fact actually influences your decisions in the moment.

Most of what we know about our finances is the first kind. You know your salary. You know roughly what your hourly rate is. You can calculate, on demand, what an hour of your time is "worth." But that knowledge sits in a different mental folder from the one that decides whether to spend forty minutes arguing about a $12 refund.

This gap has a name in the literature: the salience effect. People consistently behave as if hidden costs don't exist, even when they intellectually know they do. The classic example is the tip jar versus the tip line — same money, but the visible version makes people give twice as much. Hotel "resort fees" hidden until checkout get charged at much higher rates than the same amount tacked onto the room rate up front. Subscriptions you can't see bleed money you'd otherwise notice.

Time earned works the same way, in reverse. Because you don't see the money arriving — because it sits in some abstract account, deposited every two weeks, divorced from the hours that produced it — the connection between time and money becomes purely cognitive. You know it's there. You don't feel it. So it doesn't influence your moment-to-moment choices.

Knowing a number and feeling a number are two different mental events.

Five shifts that tend to happen

Once that connection becomes experiential — once the number is actually moving — several things tend to shift. These aren't promises; they're patterns we've observed and that the research literature supports.

1. Meetings start to have a cost

The most immediate effect. A 60-minute meeting with eight people in it stops being a "meeting" and becomes a measurable event with a dollar figure. People who have used Pulse for a few days often say they start declining meetings they would have automatically accepted. Not from spite — from a clearer sense of the trade being made.

2. Wait times become tangible

The 25 minutes on hold with the bank. The 90 minutes lost to a flight delay. The hour spent figuring out a hotel website. These are no longer just "annoying." They have a price. This often shifts behavior toward delegation, automation, or — sometimes — simply accepting that the thing isn't worth fighting for.

3. Small purchases get re-weighted

The classic move: "Is this $14 cocktail worth half an hour of work?" Sometimes yes. Sometimes no. The point isn't to deprive yourself — it's to make the trade conscious. Most people report buying fewer things, but enjoying the things they do buy more.

4. Rest gets re-valued

This is the surprising one. You might expect that seeing your earnings tick up would make resting feel costly — every hour off is "lost income." In practice, the effect is the opposite. Because rest is now visibly cheap (you're not earning, but you're also not spending), it stops feeling guilty. Walks, naps, time with family — these are the highest-value hours of the day, because they cost nothing and produce something you can't buy back.

5. Big decisions become easier

The job offer with the higher salary but longer commute. The "great opportunity" that requires weekend work. The promotion that comes with two extra hours of meetings a day. These decisions are notoriously hard to evaluate in the abstract. Once you can run them through a real hourly rate calculation, they often simplify in surprising ways.

The thing this isn't

It's worth saying clearly what this tool is not for, because the framing matters.

It is not for squeezing more productivity out of yourself. The world is saturated with apps that promise to make you faster, more focused, more efficient. Pulse is not one of them. Watching your salary tick up while you grind through more tasks is not the point.

It is also not for optimizing every minute. If you start feeling guilty for taking a long lunch because you "could have been earning," the tool is making your life worse, not better. Close the tab. Take the lunch.

The intended effect is closer to what the meditation teacher Joseph Goldstein calls knowing the situation — not changing it, just seeing it clearly. The price tag exists whether you watch it or not. The hours pass whether you count them or not. Pulse just makes one of these things briefly visible, in case the visibility is useful.

A note on attention

One genuine risk of any visibility tool is that it captures attention disproportionate to its value. If you spend more time watching the counter than working, you've broken the very thing it's measuring.

The honest recommendation: glance at it. Notice what hour of work just cost you. Then put it away. Most people who get value from Pulse use it the way they'd use a clock — frequently but briefly. The point is the calibration, not the staring.

What you're calibrating, ultimately, is your sense of what the trade actually is. The salary number is one half of a deal you make with your employer. The hours are the other half. Most of us know the first half by heart and have no felt sense of the second.

Watching the trade happen in real time, even for a few minutes a day, can rebalance that — and once it does, the small decisions and the big ones both start to look a little different.
